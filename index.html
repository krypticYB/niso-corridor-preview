WALL 01 Entry Corridor — Left Wing Historical Foundation

Wall 01 — Origins

Origins of Electricity in Nigeria

The story begins in colonial Lagos. From a single generating plant on Marina Street to a statutory national framework — the foundation of Nigeria's electricity infrastructure was laid over five decades.

First electric supply in Nigeria

Electricity was introduced in Lagos in 1896 by the colonial Public Works Department. A small generating plant at Marina powered streetlights and government offices. Access was limited and primarily served colonial administration. This marked the birth of electricity in Nigeria.

A national electricity framework

The Electricity Corporation of Nigeria (ECN) was established under Ordinance No. 15 of 1950. For the first time, electricity generation and distribution were organized under a statutory national framework. Power supply began expanding beyond Lagos into other regions.

Harnessing hydropower

The Niger Dams Authority (NDA) was established under the Niger Dams Act of 1962. It led to the construction of the Kainji Dam — completed 1968 — making hydropower a cornerstone of Nigeria's electricity supply.

Wall 02 — Development

National Development

As national ambitions expanded, electricity moved from a limited colonial service into a development instrument. ECN and NDA merged into a single national authority — consolidating generation, transmission, and distribution under one institution.

ECN

Institutional build-out

The Electricity Corporation of Nigeria became a central point of coordination, helping shape an organized national supply structure.

1972

NEPA is formed

ECN and the Niger Dams Authority were merged to form the National Electric Power Authority, consolidating generation and power delivery under one institution.

Development era

From service provision to national utility identity

In this phase, electricity becomes inseparable from modernization, industrial growth, and public service delivery. The corridor wall presents this as a move from fragmented beginnings to national-scale ambition.

WALL 03 Mid Corridor — Left Wing Reform & Market Structure

Wall 03 — Reform

Reform & Unbundling

The EPSR Act 2005 dismantled the state monopoly and created a regulated multi-actor market. Generation, transmission, and distribution became distinct institutional functions for the first time.

National Electric Power Policy

The National Electric Power Policy launched a formal reform pathway and set the stage for deeper institutional restructuring.

EPSR Act and PHCN era

The Electric Power Sector Reform Act established the legal framework for a restructured electricity market, while PHCN became a transitional institutional vehicle.

Privatization and unbundling

Generation and distribution moved into privatized structures, while transmission remained government-owned. The sector framework became more visibly segmented into GENCOs, DISCOs, and TCN.

Wall 05 — The Birth of NISO

Emergence of NISO

For the first time in Nigeria's electricity history, system operation was institutionally separated from transmission ownership. A structural shift toward neutrality, transparency, and enhanced grid reliability.

2023

Electricity Act 2023

The Electricity Act 2023 repealed the EPSR Act and introduced a more liberalized electricity framework — providing for functional separation of transmission and system operation, and decentralization allowing states to generate and distribute electricity.

2023

NISO established

Under the Electricity Act 2023, NISO was created to independently manage System Operations, Market Operations, grid reliability, and transparent market settlement.

Today

Operational independence

NISO stands at the centre of Nigeria's electricity transformation — ensuring power flows reliably, markets operate transparently, and the grid remains stable for generations to come.
