01

Origins of Electricity

Pending Approval

📍 Corridor entry — first wall visible on approach

Establishes the scientific foundation of electricity, from Franklin (1752) to Tesla (1888), grounding the Nigerian story within a global legacy of discovery.

Benjamin Franklin — Understanding Nature's Power

Franklin's 1752 kite experiment demonstrated that lightning is electrical. His work introduced the concept of positive and negative charges and opened the field of scientific electrical inquiry.

Volta, Ohm & Faraday — The Building Blocks

Volta's 1800 voltaic pile produced the first continuous current. Ohm formalized circuit mathematics in 1827. Faraday's 1831 discovery of electromagnetic induction led directly to the electric generator.

Edison & Tesla — Powering the Modern World

Edison commercialized the light bulb and built the first power stations in 1879. Tesla and Westinghouse's AC transmission system (1888) made long-distance electricity delivery possible — the foundation of every modern grid.

Content for this wall
  • Portrait panels: Franklin, Volta, Ohm, Faraday, Edison, Tesla
  • Timeline band spanning 1752 to 1888
  • Section headline: "From Lightning to Current — The Science Behind the Grid"
02

Electricity Arrives in Nigeria

Pending Approval

📍 Corridor — second panel, left side

Documents the introduction of electricity to Nigeria from 1896 through the establishment of the national framework in 1950, marking the transition from colonial provision to statutory governance.

1896

First electric supply in Nigeria

Electricity was introduced in Lagos by the colonial Public Works Department. A generating plant at Marina powered streetlights and government offices — the birth of electricity in Nigeria.

1950

Electricity Corporation of Nigeria

ECN was established under Ordinance No. 15 of 1950 — the first statutory national framework for electricity generation and distribution. Power supply began expanding beyond Lagos into the regions.

Wall narrative

From colonial provision to national institution

Fifty-four years separate a streetlamp at Marina from a national electricity ordinance. This wall presents that transition as the first act of Nigeria's electricity story.

03

National Development & NEPA

Pending Approval

📍 Corridor — mid-section, spanning full wall width

Covers the infrastructure build-out era: the Niger Dams Authority, Kainji Dam, and the 1972 formation of NEPA as a vertically integrated national monopoly.

Niger Dams Authority established

The NDA was created under the Niger Dams Act of 1962, signaling Nigeria's commitment to large-scale hydropower infrastructure as a national development priority.

Kainji Dam completed

Completed in 1968, the Kainji Dam became the cornerstone of Nigeria's electricity supply — hydropower at national scale, serving as a physical symbol of post-independence ambition.

NEPA — National Electric Power Authority

ECN and NDA were merged under the NEPA Act in 1972. NEPA became a vertically integrated monopoly responsible for generation, transmission, and distribution nationwide under central control.

04

Reform & Unbundling

Pending Approval

📍 Corridor — right side, approaching the main office zone

Documents the structural dismantling of the NEPA monopoly — the EPSR Act 2005, creation of NERC, formation of GenCos and DisCos, and the 2013 privatization that created the modern NESI.

EPSR Act 2005 — Restructuring the sector

The Electric Power Sector Reform Act unbundled NEPA and created NERC, Generation Companies, Distribution Companies, TCN, and NBET. PHCN was established as the transitional structure toward privatization.

Privatization — 1 November 2013

PHCN successor companies were privatized. Nigeria transitioned from a state monopoly to a structured market: independent GenCos, a government-owned TCN, privatized DisCos, and a regulated electricity market — the birth of the modern NESI.

NERC GenCos DisCos TCN NBET

05

The Emergence of NISO

Pending Approval

📍 Corridor — centrepiece wall, maximum dwell point

The institutional climax of the corridor. This wall presents NISO's mandate, legislative basis, and functional separation from TCN — the moment Nigeria's electricity system gained an independent operator.

Why this wall matters

Every wall before this one describes inheritance. This wall describes transformation. NISO is not a continuation of what came before — it is a structural shift: system operation separated from asset ownership, market neutrality institutionalized, and grid reliability made an independent mandate. For the first time in Nigeria's electricity history, no single entity controls both the infrastructure and the operations. That is the significance of this wall.

2023 Act

Electricity Act 2023

Repealed the EPSR Act. Provided for functional separation of transmission and system operation, and established the legal basis for NISO as an independent entity.

Mandate

Four independent functions

System Operations. Market Operations. Grid reliability and stability. Transparent market settlement. All four institutionally separated from transmission ownership for the first time.

Today

NISO operational

NISO stands at the coordination layer of Nigeria's modern electricity market — ensuring power flows reliably, markets operate transparently, and the grid remains stable.

NISO institutional mandate

Reliability — Neutrality — Transparency — Market Discipline — Grid Confidence
